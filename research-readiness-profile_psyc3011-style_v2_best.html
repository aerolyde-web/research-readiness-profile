SPINE (THE BIG QUESTION)
i read my undergraduate work as repeated attempts to answer one larger question: how do we build tools, interventions, or explanations about human minds without being fooled by simplistic claims?
that is also my psychology × ai bridge: i’m interested in how complex behaviour gets measured, modelled, and acted on responsibly when systems are noisy and individual differences matter—without mistaking proxies for understanding.
three stable obsessions that recur across the work: (a) conditionality (when does it work, and for whom?), (b) validity (does the measure/construct justify the claim?), (c) translation (how do we turn insight into something usable without flattening reality?).
research identity (fast read)
across my work in psychology and neuroscience, the recurring theme is not brain or behaviour in the abstract, but how complex human processes can be studied, measured, and acted on without collapsing into simplistic claims. again and again, my projects return to the same problems: when an effect holds, for whom it holds, whether the measure can genuinely detect it, and how conclusions should change under real-world constraints.
this is what draws me toward psychology × ai: building and evaluating psychologically informed systems that are adaptive rather than one-size-fits-all, methodologically serious rather than overconfident, and useful without pretending that proxies are the same as understanding.
recurring themes (what keeps reappearing)
  • people are not interchangeable: moderation, interaction effects, targeting, boundary conditions
  • the measure shapes the conclusion: validity, sensitivity, confounds, construct/proxy separation
  • translation under constraints: honest inference with attrition, adherence, and real-world data quality limits
  • levels of explanation: conceptual precision; what is logically legitimate to claim

signature anchors (3–5 projects that carry the identity)

1) when design interventions help vs harm (conditionality)

method: 2×2×2 factorial (working memory capacity × prompts × layout) testing when “seductive details” change comprehension.
takeaway: learner–design alignment; interaction patterns matter more than main effects.

2) contradictory findings as a measurement problem (validity)

method: sleep consolidation + recognition sensitivity work designed around confounds (delay, learning time, list properties).
takeaway: if the measure can’t detect the effect, the literature looks inconsistent; validity is the hinge of inference.

3) educationally useful effects depend on processing fit (conditionality → translation)

method: testing-effect work framed as acquisition–retrieval processing alignment across materials and retrieval formats.
takeaway: mechanism + boundary conditions; “what works” depends on what is being learned.

4) applied evidence under constraints (translation)

method: behavioural/CBT‑I style intervention evaluation with attrition + validity thresholds (e.g., minimum diary entries).
takeaway: honest interpretation under real-world data quality constraints; usefulness without false confidence.

5) conceptual precision (levels of explanation)

method: mereological fallacy analysis: keeping neuro/psych claims logically legitimate.
takeaway: conceptual hygiene as methodological hygiene (prevents overclaiming).
