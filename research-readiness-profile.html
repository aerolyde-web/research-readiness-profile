Research identity

I work at the point where messy questions about mind, brain, and behaviour have to become defensible claims. Across my undergraduate research writing, the recurring move is methodological: specify what would count as evidence, build designs that can detect the relevant boundary conditions (rather than chasing “main effects”), interrogate measurement validity and confounds, and communicate conclusions proportionately—clear enough to survive scrutiny without overclaiming.

I am preparing for psychology Honours and longer-term clinical/research training by developing a research practice that is interaction-aware (when an effect holds, and for whom), validity-first (whether the measure can support the inference), and translationally careful (what the result implies—and what it doesn’t).

Current focus (recurring themes)

  • Boundary conditions over universals: when interventions help versus harm, and what learner/context factors moderate effects.
  • Validity and sensitivity as the hinge of inference: recognising when contradictory literatures reflect weak operationalisation, insensitive outcomes, or design confounds.
  • Evidence that stays honest under constraints: interpreting applied/intervention data with attrition, adherence thresholds, and imperfect measures without inflating conclusions.
  • Levels of explanation and claim discipline: keeping psychological and neuroscientific statements logically well-formed (what can be attributed to brains, persons, tasks, or systems).

Core research capabilities

  • Interaction / moderation logic: designing for “for whom / when” rather than one-size-fits-all effects.
  • Factorial experimental thinking: translating mechanism-level hypotheses into multi-factor tests where the interaction pattern is the claim.
  • Validity-first interpretation: treating measures and confounds as part of the argument; writing within the method’s inference limits.
  • Literature synthesis under disagreement: explaining contradictions through operationalisation, timing, stimulus properties, and sensitivity.
  • Proportionate scientific writing: clear claims, calibrated conclusions, and concrete follow-up tests to resolve ambiguity.
  • Computational/AI-supported workflow (cautious): using tools to support reproducible analysis and stimulus generation with verification.

Selected signature work

When do design “improvements” help learning—and when do they backfire?

factorial design • interaction effects • learner–design alignment

Problem:
Educational design advice is often treated as universal best practice despite learner differences and material constraints.
Method:
2×2×2 factorial experiment (working memory capacity × instructional prompts × layout) testing context-dependent effects of “seductive details”.
Insight:
The meaningful outcome is not “good vs bad design” but conditional effects: the same intervention changes value depending on cognitive resources and presentation structure.
What it shows:
Comfort with interaction-heavy designs and supervisor-legible boundary condition reasoning.

Why do literatures conflict? Sometimes the measure cannot see the effect.

measurement validity • confounds • sensitivity

Problem:
Sleep–memory findings can appear contradictory when outcomes are insensitive or designs are confounded by timing/material properties.
Method:
Recognition-memory study designed around test delay, learning time, and list properties; used controlled list construction (incl. GloVe/AI-generated stimuli) to probe mechanism limits.
Insight:
Contradictions often reflect validity and sensitivity limits (recognition vs recall demands; stimulus structure) rather than genuine theoretical disagreement.
What it shows:
Validity-first instincts and disciplined interpretation under uncertainty.

The “testing effect” isn’t a slogan: benefits depend on processing alignment.

mechanism-first reasoning • interaction logic • educational translation

Problem:
Retrieval practice is broadly recommended, but usefulness depends on how acquisition and retrieval formats match the structure of material.
Method:
Work examining processing–retrieval match across different text structures and retrieval formats, with attention to interaction patterns and mechanism-level interpretation.
Insight:
“Testing helps” becomes defensible only when the retrieval demand is aligned with the representational structure being learned.
What it shows:
Ability to compress cognitive theory into bounded, usable principles without universalising.

Evidence under real-world constraints: evaluating a behavioural sleep intervention honestly.

applied reasoning • attrition • validity thresholds

Problem:
Behavioural interventions face attrition, adherence variability, and imperfect self-report, yet still require defensible inference.
Method:
Analysis of a modified self-administered CBT‑I / behavioural sleep program using pragmatic validity thresholds (e.g., minimum diary entries) and transparent interpretation under missingness.
Insight:
Responsible conclusions are bounded: what improved, where data are interpretable, and what remains uncertain given adherence and measurement limits.
What it shows:
Clinically adjacent evidence reasoning with methodological transparency.

Conceptual precision as methodological hygiene: keeping claims logically legitimate.

levels of explanation • philosophy of science • claim discipline

Problem:
Neuroscience and psychology can drift into category mistakes (e.g., attributing person-level predicates to brain parts), which muddies what studies can legitimately claim.
Method:
Philosophy of science analysis of the mereological fallacy, arguing for clearer levels-of-explanation in psychological and neuroscientific explanation.
Insight:
Conceptual clarity is not decoration; it shapes coherent hypotheses and prevents overinterpretation.
What it shows:
Care for the logic of scientific claims alongside statistical and experimental competence.

Honours direction / fit

I am best suited to Honours environments that value experimental design, interaction/moderation reasoning, measurement validity, and careful inference—especially where questions sit at the junction of cognition/learning, behavioural and cognitive neuroscience, and clinically meaningful outcomes. I look for projects where methodological decisions are treated as theory decisions: not just “run a study,” but build evidence that remains credible under scrutiny.

Alternate opening (optional)

Alternate subtitle: a methodological research profile for psychology Honours

My through-line is methodological rather than topical: I focus on how claims about mind, brain, and behaviour are made defensible—by designing for boundary conditions, choosing measures that can genuinely detect the phenomenon, treating confounds as part of the theory, and communicating conclusions in proportion to the evidence.
